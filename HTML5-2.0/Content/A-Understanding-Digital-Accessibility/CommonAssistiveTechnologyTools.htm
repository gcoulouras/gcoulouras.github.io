Common Assistive Technology Tools

Assistive Technology (AT) is defined as an item or software that is specifically designed to assist disabled users and help them easily navigate the web1 “Disability.” Adobe Inclusive Design Workshop, Adobe, 2020. PDF. . In the context of web accessibility, professional writers should understand AT tools to design their content for people who use them.

One such tool is a screen reader, which is a form of assistive technology that reads a computer screen, most commonly in audio form, for a person who is blind or has low vision. A screen reader can also output information through a braille display. Since screen readers are widely used by people of all abilities, it is integral to design content that is compatible with screen readers, like including alt text on all images. Alt text is text that is read aloud by a screen reader in place of an image, and is usually a short description of the image. It should not be mistaken for a caption, and in fact, would be repetitive if the alt text and caption displayed the same message, as it would just be read by the screen reader twice.

Screen magnifiers, which zoom in on a specific area of the screen, are often used in conjunction with a screen reader. This allows users to avoid scrolling horizontally and vertically, as the magnifier often hides other important information when zoomed in.

Speech recognition technology allows a person to operate a computer or mobile device through a microphone using spoken commands. This technology assists people with motor disabilities, such as cerebral palsy, letting them operate a device hands-free2 “Kalbag, Laura. “Considering Accessibility.” Accessibility for Everyone, Small Technology Foundation, 2017, https://accessibilityforeveryone.site/considering-accessibility/.. As we discussed, speech-to-text recognition has become embedded into everyday technology use through voice assistants such as Siri.

For video content, including closed captions is essential for viewers who are hearing impaired. Closed captions synchronize audio content into a visible text form, and can also relay sounds that aren’t text, like a whisper or a loud noise. However, do not just rely on auto-generated captions, as these can often be inaccurate. Edit the captions yourself if necessary. Unlike closed captions that display text on-screen as it appears in the audio, transcripts display the entirety of an audio in text format. Both are integral features for making audio content accessible.
