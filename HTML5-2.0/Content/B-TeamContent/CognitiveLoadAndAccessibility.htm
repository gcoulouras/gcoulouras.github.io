Cognitive Load and Accessibility

When it comes to accessibility in the digital world, the ability to receive the information is what drives the usage of your website. Having an attractive aesthetic and engaging content isn’t enough to make a website receivable – the key is keeping the user’s mental load down. For many readers, reducing cognitive load is a nice plus for a website, but for many, this is an accessibility requirement.

Making sure your website is functioning requires more than just good coding – it requires website accessibility.

What is cognitive load?

Cognitive load refers to the amount of information our working memory can process at any time. In other words, the amount of brain power that you have to complete a task can be referred to as your “cognitive load”. Being exhausted from using too much cognitive energy can be not only frustrating, but even physically exhausting1 https://watershednotes.ca/2020/03/04/cognitive-load-in-mental-health/..

Cognitive load in relation to accessibility and digital spaces

Cognitive disabilities can encompass a broad range of conditions that may require additional accessibility measures to lighten mental strain.

These conditions can impair functions such as2 https://www.section508.gov/design/digital-content-users-with-cognitive-disabilities/.:

  • Memory

  • Attention

  • Perception

  • Learning

  • Problem Solving

  • Comprehension

This can be a result of a temporary condition, such as a brain injury, a permanent condition, such as ADHD2 – but, even individuals without specific cognitive disabilities often find themselves cognitively overloaded. Factors like illness, fatigue, stress, or distraction can impair anyone’s cognitive function, despite their cognitive health3 https://vispero.com/resources/making-the-web-accessible-a-focus-on-cognitive-disabilities/#:~:text=As%20more%20organizations%20strive%20to,disabilities%20might%20face%20challenges%20with: .

When it comes to digital spaces, like websites, accessibility must be at the forefront. Websites that aren’t designed with cognitive accessibility are not only frustrating for users (especially those with cognitive disabilities), but for some, become simply unusable3.

It’s important to recognize that the digital landscape is more than just aesthetics. For those who are disabled, a digital space that is cognitively overstimulating misshapes how users interact with digital spaces and process information in a web-dependent world.

Reducing cognitive load in a digital space

Web designers have the responsibility of incorporating inclusive design in their websites and apps. Luckily, there are many guidelines that help outline the rules of digital accessibility and resources that can provide insights.

Reducing cognitive load: text2

To avoid cognitive load, implementing texts of webpages must be intentional. Examples of intentional, accessible text include:

  • Avoiding jargon and using plain, straightforward text, overcrowding screens with text, and making sure to present information in manageable sections.

  • Implementing alternative text (image descriptions) and descriptive text/labels for icons.

To ease user experience, websites should:

  • Provide clear user error messaging and avoid ambiguous error screens to reduce confusion when running across website issues.

  • Include visual and auditory formats to supplement/replace text if text does not show up, or for those who cannot read text.

  • Include captions, audio descriptions, and transcript for videos.

Reducing cognitive load: design3

Just like text choices must be intentional, design choices should be equally as calculated. Examples of intentional, accessible design include:

  • Using readable fonts such as sans-serif, a large font size, sufficient line and text spacing, and ensure sufficient color contrast between text and elements.

  • Including visual cues, such as designing a navigation that is predictable.

  • Allow customization of text sizing, colors, interaction speed, etc.

  • Avoid flashy animations or flashing elements.

Making sure a digital space is not only needed for accessibility purposes, but there are also requirements that are legally protected. Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) are rules that are required for a website to be considered accessible. There are 3 levels of accessibility4 https://www.wcag.com/blog/web-content-accessibility-guidelines-wcag-by-the-numbers/ 5 https://www.levelaccess.com/blog/ada-compliance-levels/ ,:

  • Level A – Most basic level of accessibility.

  • Level AA – More accessible, addresses biggest and most common accessibility issues.

  • Level AAA – Highest level of accessibility and is sometimes not possible to reach for certain sites.

This is a high level overview of accessibility measures that aid in digital accessibility and reduce cognitive load; however, visiting a vetted website such as WC3 or Section508.gov is a helpful way for designers to ensure they are checking all the boxes.

Assistive technology to reduce cognitive load

An accessible website not only broadens your audience but also ensures your organization’s message reaches everyone effectively. Not only that, but legally, there are certain standards you must adhere to.

Accessibility can only go so far – sometimes, assistive technology (ATs), a device or software that helps those with disabilities, is needed for a person to roam digitally and reduce cognitive load.

Video and audio ATs6 https://reciteme.com/us/news/assistive-technology-for-people-with-hearing-impairments/ 7 https://reciteme.com/us/news/assistive-technology-for-adhd/ 8 https://op.europa.eu/en/web/accessibility/transcript-concept-assistive-technology

  • Video players that display captions. This can include transcripts of the video, captions, and subtitles that render any audio information from audio to text. This should include options for different languages.

    • Transcripts: A text version of the video.

    • Captions: synchronized text that appears on the screen while the video plays, which includes dialogue, speaker identification, and sound effects. Closed captions can be turned on or off, open captions cannot be turned off.

  • Software equalizers can be used to amplify or attenuate different frequency bands of an audio signal to improve the user’s perception. In other words, a user can regulate how loud audio is playing in one ear versus another ear.

  • Assistive listening devices can filter out sounds, or amplify sounds. This can be used to hear videos better and reduce background noise.

  • Noise-cancelling headphones can help create a controlled environment and reduce external sounds and reduce cognitive load that is related to sensory overload.

  • Screen readers can help reduce cognitive load by reducing the amount of energy associated with reading and staying focused, and help reduce distractions.
